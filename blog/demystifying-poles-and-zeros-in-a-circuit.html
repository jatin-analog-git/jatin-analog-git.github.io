Demystifying Poles and Zeros in a circuit

A step-by-step approach to analyzing the stability and frequency response of electronic circuits.

Tutorial June 22, 2026 5 min read Jatin

Introduction

Poles and zeros are fundamental concepts in circuit analysis that determine the frequency response and stability of electronic circuits. Understanding how to identify and analyze them is crucial for designing and optimizing any analog circuit.

In this tutorial, we'll explore the fundamentals of poles and zeros and demonstrate how to apply them to real-world circuit design problems.

What are Poles and Zeros?

Poles and zeros are the roots of the numerator and denominator of a transfer function, respectively. In simpler terms, they represent the frequencies at which the circuit's response becomes zero or infinite.

\[ H(s) = \frac{N(s)}{D(s)} \]

where \(N(s)\) is the numerator polynomial and \(D(s)\) is the denominator polynomial. The poles and zeros are the roots of these polynomials.

  • Short-channel effects
  • Mobility degradation
  • Threshold voltage variations
Key Insight: The gm/ID ratio provides a technology-independent measure of transistor efficiency, ranging from strong inversion (~5 V⁻¹) to weak inversion (~25 V⁻¹).

The Fundamental Relationship

The cornerstone of this methodology is the gm/ID ratio:

\[ \frac{g_m}{I_D} = \frac{2}{V_{GS} - V_{TH}} \quad \text{(strong inversion)} \]

And in weak inversion:

\[ \frac{g_m}{I_D} = \frac{q}{nkT} \approx \frac{1}{n \cdot 26\text{mV}} \]

Design Procedure

The gm/ID design flow follows these steps:

Step 1: Define Specifications

Start with your circuit requirements:

  • Required transconductance: \(g_m = 1 \text{ mS}\)
  • Available current: \(I_D = 100 \mu\text{A}\)
  • Minimum \(f_T\): 1 GHz

Step 2: Calculate Target gm/ID

\[ \frac{g_m}{I_D} = \frac{1 \text{ mS}}{100 \mu\text{A}} = 10 \text{ V}^{-1} \]

Step 4: Size the Transistor

Using the normalized current density from lookup tables:

\[ W = \frac{I_D}{I_D/W} = \frac{I_D}{\text{lookup}(g_m/I_D,\; I_D/W)} \]

Practical Example: OTA Input Stage

Let's design the input differential pair for an OTA:

ParameterValue
Input transconductance500 µS
Bias current per branch50 µA
Minimum transit frequency500 MHz

From these specifications, we get \(g_m/I_D = 10 \text{ V}^{-1}\), placing our design in moderate inversion — a good tradeoff between speed and power.

Conclusion

The gm/ID methodology provides a systematic, technology-aware approach to analog design. By using characterized device data rather than simplified models, designers can achieve better first-pass success rates and more optimal designs.

"The gm/ID methodology bridges the gap between hand calculations and simulation, enabling designers to make informed tradeoffs before committing to detailed design."
